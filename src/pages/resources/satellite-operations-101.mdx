# Satellite Operations 101

Welcome to Satellite Operations 101! This interactive guide provides a foundational understanding of satellite operations. Whether you're a designer, developer, or product owner, this document will equip you with the essential knowledge of satellite functionality and management.

## Orbits

Satellites orbit the Earth in various paths, each with its own characteristics. Understanding these orbits is crucial for effective satellite operations.

### Key Orbit Types

<InteractiveSection title="Low Earth Orbit (LEO)">
  <OrbitHighlight type="leo">
    **Altitude**: 160–2,000 km  
    **Orbital Period**: ~90 minutes  
    **Key Uses**: Earth observation, telecommunications, space stations (ISS), Starlink  
    **Advantages**: Low latency, less power for communications, easier to reach  
    **Disadvantages**: Atmospheric drag, shorter ground coverage periods, shorter lifespan
  </OrbitHighlight>
  
  Low Earth Orbit satellites zip around Earth in roughly 90-minute cycles, offering excellent responsiveness 
  for communications and high-resolution imagery. Their proximity makes them cheaper to launch and easier to service, 
  but they experience atmospheric drag that limits their operational lifespan.
</InteractiveSection>

<InteractiveSection title="Medium Earth Orbit (MEO)">
  <OrbitHighlight type="meo">
    **Altitude**: 2,000–35,786 km  
    **Orbital Period**: 2–12 hours  
    **Key Uses**: Navigation constellations (GPS, Galileo, GLONASS)  
    **Advantages**: Good balance of coverage and latency, less radiation than GEO  
    **Disadvantages**: Need more satellites than GEO for continuous coverage  
  </OrbitHighlight>
  
  MEO strikes a balance between the speedy performance of LEO and the broad coverage of GEO. 
  This makes it perfect for navigation systems like GPS, allowing precise positioning with relatively few satellites.
</InteractiveSection>

<InteractiveSection title="Geostationary Orbit (GEO)">
  <OrbitHighlight type="geo">
    **Altitude**: 35,786 km  
    **Orbital Period**: 24 hours (synchronized with Earth's rotation)  
    **Key Uses**: Broadcasting satellites, weather monitoring, some communications  
    **Advantages**: Fixed position relative to Earth, continuous coverage of large areas  
    **Disadvantages**: High latency, high radiation environment, expensive to reach  
  </OrbitHighlight>
  
  Satellites in GEO appear motionless in the sky because they orbit at exactly the same rate as Earth's rotation. 
  This perfect synchronization allows them to continuously monitor large portions of the planet
  or provide uninterrupted communication services to fixed ground antennas.
</InteractiveSection>

<InteractiveSection title="Polar Orbit">
  <OrbitHighlight type="polar">
    **Inclination**: ~90°  
    **Key Uses**: Earth observation, climate monitoring, reconnaissance  
    **Advantages**: Covers the entire Earth as it rotates, passes over poles  
    **Disadvantages**: Limited coverage time for any specific location  
  </OrbitHighlight>
  
  By passing over (or very near) both poles with each orbit, these satellites eventually see every part of Earth's surface 
  as the planet rotates underneath. This makes polar orbits ideal for mapping, monitoring climate change,
  and gathering comprehensive Earth observation data.
</InteractiveSection>

<InteractiveSection title="Sun-Synchronous Orbit (SSO)">
  <OrbitHighlight type="sso">
    **Inclination**: ~98°  
    **Key Characteristic**: Passes over any given point at the same local time  
    **Key Uses**: Earth imaging, weather forecasting  
    **Advantages**: Consistent lighting conditions for imaging, regular revisit times  
    **Disadvantages**: Limited to specific altitude/inclination combinations  
  </OrbitHighlight>
  
  A special type of near-polar orbit, SSO satellites are carefully placed so their orbital plane precesses (rotates) at the same 
  rate that Earth orbits the Sun. This creates consistent lighting conditions for Earth observation, 
  making changes over time easier to detect.
</InteractiveSection>

<InteractiveSection title="Highly Elliptical Orbit (HEO)">
  <OrbitHighlight type="heo">
    **Eccentricity**: High (>0.5)  
    **Examples**: Molniya orbit (~12 hr period, 63.4° inclination)  
    **Key Uses**: Communications for high latitudes, specialized observation  
    **Advantages**: Extended coverage of high latitudes or specific regions  
    **Disadvantages**: Complex operations, varying altitude  
  </OrbitHighlight>
  
  HEO satellites follow an elongated path, spending most of their time at high altitude over specific regions. 
  The Molniya orbit, for example, was designed to provide long dwell times over Russia and other northern areas 
  poorly served by equatorial GEO satellites.
</InteractiveSection>

## Satellite Mission Lifecycle Overview

This document traces a satellite's journey from concept through retirement, blending narrative flow with detailed definitions of key orbits, subsystems, and control concepts.

## Mission Definition & Design

Before launch, mission goals drive every technical choice:

- **Mission Objectives**: Specify coverage (global vs. regional), revisit frequency, data latency, and payload function (e.g., imaging resolution, communications bandwidth, scientific sensing).

- **Orbit Selection**: Choose an orbit to meet objectives:
  - **Low Earth Orbit (LEO)**: 160–2,000 km altitude, ~90 min orbital period, low-latency Earth observation or telecom.
  - **Medium Earth Orbit (MEO)**: 2,000–35,786 km, 2–12 hr periods (navigation constellations use ~12 hr), balanced coverage and revisit time.
  - **Geostationary Orbit (GEO)**: 35,786 km equatorial, 24 hr period, appears fixed for continuous broadcast and weather monitoring.
  - **High Earth Orbit (HEO)**: Highly elliptical (e.g., Molniya, ~12 hr period, ~40,000 km apogee) for prolonged high-latitude coverage.
  - **Sun-Synchronous Orbit (SSO)**: Near-polar, precessing to maintain constant local solar time, ideal for consistent illumination in imaging.
  - **Polar Orbit**: ~90° inclination, passes over poles each revolution, full Earth coverage as the planet rotates.

- **Payload & Subsystem Requirements**: Derive sensor specifications, data throughput, power and thermal budgets, and pointing accuracy.

- **GNC (Guidance, Navigation & Control) Specs**: Define attitude precision (arcsecond-level for high-resolution imaging) and orbit-keeping Δv budgets.

> _Transition_: With design locked in, we turn to getting the satellite on its way.

---

## Launch & Deployment

Key steps ensure the satellite reaches its intended orbit and becomes operational:

1. **Launch Vehicle & Window**
   - Select rocket family and upper stage based on payload mass and target orbit.
   - Determine launch window from orbital mechanics and weather constraints.

2. **Separation & Initial Acquisition**
   - After stage separation, deploy solar arrays and stabilize attitude.
   - Acquire initial **TT&C** (Telemetry, Tracking & Command) link for health checks.

3. **Orbit Insertion Maneuvers**
   - Execute burns to circularize or phase into the operational orbit (e.g., apogee/perigee adjustments).

> _Transition_: Once in place, maintaining path and pointing becomes the daily task.
